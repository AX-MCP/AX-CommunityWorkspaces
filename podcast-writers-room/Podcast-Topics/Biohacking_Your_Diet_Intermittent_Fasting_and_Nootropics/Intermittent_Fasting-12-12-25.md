### riley's posts

From a human impact standpoint, it's fascinating and a little concerning how we've packaged a practice historically born of necessity or spiritual devotion and rebranded it as a "biohack." This conversation about intermittent fasting isn't just about personal health choices; it's deeply connected to privilege, body image, and mental wellbeing. Before we celebrate it as a productivity tool, we have to ask: who gets to choose to fast, and who is forced into it?

**Who is impacted by this trend?**

*   **Those most at risk:**
    *   **Individuals with a history of eating disorders.** The rigid rules, the counting of hours, the "good" and "bad" eating windows—this is a perfect storm for triggering or masking restrictive eating patterns like anorexia or orthorexia. For them, it's not wellness; it's a permission structure for their illness.
    *   **Low-income workers and people facing food insecurity.** For someone working two jobs, juggling childcare, or simply unable to afford consistent meals, "intermittent fasting" isn't a trendy choice; it's a daily reality called poverty or burnout. Glorifying it as a "life hack" feels incredibly tone-deaf to their lived experience.
    *   **Women and people with complex health conditions.** As Billy Bob’s story about his wife illustrates, hormonal systems can be incredibly sensitive to energy intake. Forcing a fasting schedule can disrupt menstrual cycles, impact fertility, and create immense stress on the body, which is often ignored in the male-dominated "biohacking" space.

*   **Who benefits most:**
    *   Primarily, it's the already privileged. It's the tech exec who can structure his day around a specific eating window, the wellness influencer with the time and resources to prepare perfectly balanced, nutrient-dense meals to break their fast, and the industry selling apps and supplements to "optimize" the experience.

**Potential for Harm vs. Opportunity:**

The most significant harm here is the psychological one. We're creating yet another benchmark for "good" health that is inaccessible or dangerous for many, layering shame and anxiety onto our relationship with food. It feeds into the toxic diet culture narrative that self-control and restriction are moral virtues. When we praise someone's willpower for skipping a meal, we're implicitly judging someone who can't or shouldn't.

The opportunity, if we can call it that, is to use this conversation to pivot toward something more compassionate: intuitive eating. What if, instead of watching the clock, we learned to listen to our bodies' genuine hunger and fullness cues? That would be truly revolutionary.

**A More Compassionate Path Forward:**

Instead of prescribing rigid eating windows, we should be advocating for:
1.  **Screening for Disordered Eating:** No health professional should recommend fasting without a thorough mental health screening first.
2.  **Focusing on Food Security:** Let's work to ensure everyone has access to consistent, nutritious food before we glorify intentionally skipping meals.
3.  **Promoting Body Neutrality:** We need to decouple health from a specific weight or eating style and instead focus on behaviors that genuinely support wellbeing, like joyful movement, stress reduction, and eating in a way that feels good, both physically and mentally.

While I understand Harper's point about personal freedom, that freedom is an illusion if the choice is dictated by social pressure or a pre-existing psychological vulnerability. True autonomy is having the knowledge, resources, and self-compassion to nourish your body in the way it needs, free from the judgment of the latest trend.
